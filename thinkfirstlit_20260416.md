## Section 1: Executive Synthesis

*Current Academic Perspectives on Generative AI in UX Research Practice*

The academic literature on generative AI in UX research practice has arrived at a pivotal inflection point. Across the three domains examined — methods, impact, and governance — a consistent picture emerges: generative AI tools are already reshaping how UX researchers work, but organizational structures, professional norms, and ethical frameworks have not kept pace with tool adoption.

In the Methods domain, the strongest and most replicated finding is that generative AI meaningfully accelerates qualitative data processing — particularly transcript coding, thematic analysis, and insight synthesis — but introduces a critical challenge around transparency and researcher positionality. The most promising approaches are hybrid: human researchers retain interpretive control while AI handles volume processing and pattern flagging.

The Impact literature reveals a more contested terrain. Practitioner studies document genuine efficiency gains, but also surface deeper concerns: de-skilling, cognitive offloading, and erosion of the empathy-building processes foundational to UX research. The role asymmetry between UX researchers (who maintain calibrated skepticism) and product managers (who overestimate AI capabilities) is emerging as a structural tension in cross-functional teams.

The Governance category is where the literature is most clearly underdeveloped. Most organizations have responded through informal caution rather than formal frameworks — leaving responsibility with individual practitioners. No peer-reviewed governance framework exists built specifically for UX research practice.

Three overarching cross-category themes: (1) trust calibration cuts across all domains and is the central practical challenge; (2) the human-AI collaboration model is still being invented; (3) the structural absence of UXR-specific governance frameworks means practitioners are improvising ethical guardrails in real time.

## Section 3: How the Field Got Here

The application of AI to user research traces back to early automation of text processing tasks. The shift began around 2020–2021 when large language models demonstrated sufficient language understanding to perform interpretive tasks previously reserved for humans. ChatGPT's arrival in late 2022 acted as a catalyst, dramatically lowering the barrier to AI-assisted qualitative work. The research landscape has shifted from 'can AI do this at all?' to 'under what conditions does AI assistance improve or degrade research quality?'

| Year | Milestone | Significance |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| 2020–21 | First LLM qualitative coding experiments | Established feasibility |
| 2022 | ChatGPT launch; first practitioner experiments | Lowered barrier; first-wave experimentation |
| 2023 | Stige et al. review (65 citations); Zhang et al. framework (105 citations) | Foundational literature established |
| 2024 | Takaffoli et al. (48 citations); Lu et al. 359-paper review; Shukla de-skilling | Field shifts from feasibility to consequences |
| 2025 | Candello trust asymmetry; Pehar synthetic users review; Smith governance | Governance gaps named; hybrid models proposed |

**Terminology note:** Early papers (pre-2023) use 'AI-assisted coding' and 'automated qualitative analysis.' Post-2023 papers increasingly use 'GenAI' and 'LLM-assisted research.' Use both when searching.

## Section 4: Category Analysis

### Category 1: Methods

*How generative AI is integrated into research workflows; how AI-assisted methods affect execution quality, efficiency, and research outputs.*

#### Category Synthesis

The Methods literature is the most mature of the three categories. The central finding is consistent: generative AI demonstrably accelerates qualitative data processing — particularly transcript coding, thematic analysis, and insight synthesis — while introducing meaningful risks around interpretive fidelity and researcher dependency.

AI performs better on deductive analysis than inductive analysis. Hybrid approaches consistently outperform AI-alone or human-alone. The synthetic participants literature is the most contested frontier: viable for pilot studies but not for replacing real user research.

A persistent gap: nearly all studies test AI assistance in controlled settings. The field lacks longitudinal studies in naturalistic UX research practice.

#### Papers — Methods

### Generative AI in User Experience Design and Research: How Do UX Practitioners, Teams, and Companies Use GenAI in Industry?

*Takaffoli, M., Li, S., & Mäkelä, V. (2024). Proceedings of the 2024 ACM Designing Interactive Systems Conference.*

**Summary**

Interview study with 24 UX practitioners across multiple countries and seniority levels documenting how GenAI is actually used in UX industry practice. Three core findings: a significant lack of company-level GenAI policies (with most organizations advising informal caution); predominantly individual rather than team-based GenAI use; and a strong preference for writing-based tasks over design-focused ones. Practitioners consistently called for better GenAI training, particularly around prompt engineering and output quality evaluation.

**Relevance to GenAI in UX Research**

Establishes the empirical baseline for practitioner GenAI adoption in UX — including the governance gap, usage patterns, and training needs that subsequent research builds on.

### Category 2: Impact

*How AI tools alter research outcomes, researcher practices, team dynamics, and the nature of generated insights.*

#### Category Synthesis

The Impact literature's central tension is between the productivity gains GenAI demonstrably provides and the less visible costs — on researcher skill, research quality, and team dynamics. The most striking theme is the risk of de-skilling and cognitive offloading.

Trust dynamics represent a second major theme — healthy practitioner skepticism is being undermined by organizational pressure. Team and social dynamics are an emerging and underexplored sub-area. A consistent gap: the absence of longitudinal research tracking how practitioner expertise evolves.

Hybrid Wizard-of-Oz study with 24 UX evaluators testing proactive AI suggestions at three timing points. ## Section 6: Open Questions & Gaps

### Methodological Gaps

- **No longitudinal research** — The field lacks multi-year studies tracking how researcher expertise, insight quality, and professional identity evolve with sustained AI use. Without this, we cannot distinguish productive skill extension from problematic skill erosion.
- **Lab vs. naturalistic settings** — Most studies use controlled or experimental settings. Research in naturalistic UX practice contexts is rare.
- **No insight quality metrics** — The field lacks agreed-upon standards for measuring whether AI assistance improves or degrades research output quality beyond efficiency measures.

### Population/Context Gaps

- **Non-Western and non-English-language contexts** — GenAI tools are largely English-language; their performance in non-English UXR contexts is almost entirely unstudied.
- **Small research teams and solo practitioners** — Independent UX researchers and small teams — who often lack organizational support for governance — are underrepresented.
- **Domain-specific UX contexts** — How AI assistance performs in specialized contexts (healthcare UX, safety-critical systems, accessibility research) is not well understood.

### Conceptual/Theoretical Gaps

- **No UXR-specific governance framework** — The field's most urgent practical gap. Practitioners are improvising ethical guardrails in real time.
- **The researcher role under sustained AI adoption** — What does UXR expertise look like when AI handles volume processing? How do researchers maintain interpretive capabilities?
- **Trust calibration as a skill** — Multiple papers identify appropriate AI trust as critical, but no model exists for how this skill develops or how to train for it.
