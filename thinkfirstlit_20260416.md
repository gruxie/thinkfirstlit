## Section 1: Executive Synthesis

*Current Academic Perspectives on Generative AI in UX Research Practice*

The academic literature on generative AI in UX research practice has arrived at a pivotal inflection point. Across the three domains examined — methods, impact, and governance — a consistent picture emerges: generative AI tools are already reshaping how UX researchers work, but organizational structures, professional norms, and ethical frameworks have not kept pace with tool adoption.

In the Methods domain, the strongest and most replicated finding is that generative AI meaningfully accelerates qualitative data processing — particularly transcript coding, thematic analysis, and insight synthesis — but introduces a critical challenge around transparency and researcher positionality. The most promising approaches are hybrid: human researchers retain interpretive control while AI handles volume processing and pattern flagging.

The Impact literature reveals a more contested terrain. Practitioner studies document genuine efficiency gains, but also surface deeper concerns: de-skilling, cognitive offloading, and the erosion of the empathy-building processes foundational to UX research. The role asymmetry between UX researchers (who maintain calibrated skepticism about AI outputs) and product managers (who tend to overestimate AI capabilities) is emerging as a structural tension in cross-functional teams.

The Governance category is where the literature is most clearly underdeveloped relative to the urgency of the problem. Most organizations have responded to generative AI adoption through informal caution rather than formal frameworks — leaving responsibility with individual practitioners. No peer-reviewed governance framework exists built specifically for UX research practice.

Three cross-category themes: (1) trust calibration cuts across all domains and is the central practical challenge; (2) the human-AI collaboration model is still being invented, with no consensus on optimal division of cognitive labor; (3) the structural absence of UXR-specific governance frameworks means practitioners are improvising ethical guardrails in real time.

## Section 3: How the Field Got Here

The application of AI to user research traces back to early automation of text processing tasks. The shift began around 2020–2021 when large language models demonstrated sufficient language understanding to perform interpretive tasks previously reserved for humans. The arrival of ChatGPT in late 2022 acted as a catalyst, dramatically lowering the barrier to AI-assisted qualitative work and generating a wave of empirical research in 2023–2024. The field is now actively constructing hybrid human-AI frameworks, with the most sophisticated 2025 work examining not just capability but consequences.

| Year | Milestone | Significance |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| 2020–21 | First LLM qualitative coding experiments | Established feasibility question |
| 2022 | ChatGPT launch; first practitioner experiments | Lowered barrier; first-wave experimentation |
| 2023 | Stige et al. review (65 citations); Zhang et al. framework (105 citations) | Foundational literature; thematic analysis AI goes mainstream |
| 2024 | Takaffoli et al. (48 citations); Lu et al. 359-paper review; Shukla et al. de-skilling | Field shifts from 'can AI do this?' to 'what are the consequences?' |
| 2025 | Candello trust asymmetry; Pehar synthetic users review; Smith governance | Governance gaps named; hybrid models proposed as standard |

**Terminology note:** Early papers (pre-2023) use 'AI-assisted coding' and 'automated qualitative analysis.' Post-2023 papers increasingly use 'GenAI' and 'LLM-assisted research.' Use both when searching foundational literature.

## Section 4: Category Analysis

### Category 1: Methods

*How generative AI is integrated into research workflows; how AI-assisted methods affect execution quality, efficiency, and research outputs.*

#### Category Synthesis

The Methods literature is the most mature of the three categories. The central finding is consistent: generative AI demonstrably accelerates qualitative data processing — particularly transcript coding, thematic analysis, and cross-participant synthesis — while introducing meaningful risks around interpretive fidelity, transparency, and researcher dependency.

The most replicated finding is that AI performs better on deductive analysis (applying pre-defined codes to data) than on inductive analysis (generating new codes from raw data). AI-assisted cluster analysis and insight synthesis shows early promise. The synthetic participants literature is the most contested frontier — viable for pilot studies and hypothesis generation but not for replacing real user research.

A methodological gap: nearly all studies test AI assistance in controlled or experimental settings. The field lacks longitudinal studies of AI-assisted methods in naturalistic UX research practice.

### Generative AI in User Experience Design and Research: How Do UX Practitioners, Teams, and Companies Use GenAI in Industry?

*Takaffoli, M., et al. (2024). Proceedings of the 2024 ACM Designing Interactive Systems Conference.*

**Summary**

This interview study with 24 UX practitioners across multiple companies and countries documents how GenAI is actually used in UX industry practice. Three core findings: a significant lack of company-level GenAI policies (with most organizations advising informal caution); predominantly individual rather than team-based GenAI use; and a strong preference for writing-based tasks over design-focused ones. Practitioners consistently called for better GenAI training, particularly around prompt engineering and output quality evaluation. Country and seniority variation in adoption patterns suggests the field is heterogeneous rather than uniformly advanced.

**Relevance to GenAI in UX Research**

Establishes the empirical baseline for practitioner GenAI adoption in UX — including the governance gap, usage patterns, and training needs that subsequent research builds on.

### Redefining Qualitative Analysis in the AI Era: Utilizing ChatGPT for Efficient Thematic Analysis

*Zhang, H., et al. (2023). ArXiv.*

**Summary**

Through semi-structured interviews with 17 participants and collaboration with 13 qualitative researchers, this study documents a notable attitudinal shift: researchers who initially approach AI-assisted qualitative analysis with skepticism tend to develop more positive views through guided experimentation. The paper develops a prompt design framework for thematic analysis with ChatGPT, finding that improving transparency, providing structured guidance, and strengthening researchers' conceptual understanding of LLM capabilities are the key levers for effective use. Ethical risks — particularly the risk of novice researchers uncritically accepting AI-generated themes — are explicitly flagged.

**Relevance to GenAI in UX Research**

### AI as Synthetic Users in Human-Centred Design: A Systematic Review

*Pehar, F. (2025). 2025 MIPRO 48th ICT and Electronics Convention.*

**Summary**

A PRISMA-guided systematic review of 52 peer-reviewed studies examining how LLMs and agent-based simulations function as synthetic users across different phases of human-centered design. The review identifies four main application themes: AI-generated personas and user profiles; AI support for ideation and prototyping; simulated users for usability testing; and agent-based system validation. While synthetic users can significantly accelerate early design iterations, consistent findings across studies raise concerns about authenticity, bias, and ethical validity. The proposed resolution is a hybrid evaluation model in which AI-driven techniques augment rather than replace real user input.

**Relevance to GenAI in UX Research**

Provides the most comprehensive evidence base for where synthetic AI participants can legitimately supplement UX research practice and where they cannot.

### CoAIcoder: Examining the Effectiveness of AI-assisted Human-to-Human Collaboration in Qualitative Analysis

*Gao, J., et al. (2023). ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction.*

**Summary**

A controlled between-subjects study with 32 pairs of participants testing four AI-assisted collaboration methods in qualitative analysis. Using a shared AI model as a mediator can improve efficiency and accelerate agreement in early coding stages, but reduces final code diversity. The study draws important distinctions between different collaboration contexts — particularly the risk of homogenizing interpretations when a shared AI model mediates between co-researchers. The finding that independence levels need to be carefully considered when designing AI-assisted collaborative QA has direct relevance to UX team research practice.

**Relevance to GenAI in UX Research**

Surfaces a non-obvious risk of AI-mediated collaborative UX research: efficiency gains may come at the cost of interpretive diversity, narrowing the range of insights generated.

### Category 2: Impact

*How AI tools alter research outcomes, researcher practices, team dynamics, and the nature of generated insights.*

#### Category Synthesis

The Impact literature's central tension is between the productivity gains GenAI demonstrably provides and the less visible costs it may impose — on researcher skill, research quality, and team dynamics.

The most striking theme is de-skilling and cognitive offloading. Shukla et al. (2025) map practitioner concerns onto established automation ironies, finding that AI assistance with divergent tasks may erode the empathy-building and pattern-recognition capabilities that are the core competency of experienced researchers. Trust dynamics represent a second major theme — healthy skepticism is being undermined by organizational pressure from PMs who overestimate AI. Team and social dynamics are an emerging and underexplored sub-area. A consistent gap: the absence of longitudinal research.

### Enhancing UX Evaluation Through Collaboration with Conversational AI Assistants: Effects of Proactive Dialogue and Timing

*Kuang, E., et al. (2024). Proceedings of the 2024 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems.*

**Summary**

A hybrid Wizard-of-Oz study with 24 UX evaluators tests the effect of proactive AI suggestions at three timing points (before, during, and after potential usability problems are identified). While timing did not significantly affect analytical performance, post-hoc suggestions were preferred and enhanced trust. Crucially, participants collectively identified more than twice as many usability problems as the AI alone, underscoring the irreplaceable role of human expertise. The study provides specific design guidance for AI-assisted UX evaluation tools around timing and framing of suggestions.

**Relevance to GenAI in UX Research**

Provides empirical evidence that AI-assisted UX evaluation works best as a complementary tool following human judgment rather than preceding or replacing it.

### Category 3: Governance

*Emerging organizational policies, ethical frameworks, quality controls, and professional responsibilities for AI-assisted research.*

#### Category Synthesis

The Governance category is the thinnest of the three in UXR-specific literature — which is itself the most significant finding. Most organizations have responded to GenAI adoption through informal caution rather than formal frameworks. The governance work that exists either addresses general organizational AI adoption or comes from adjacent domains (healthcare AI, academic research integrity). No peer-reviewed governance framework exists built specifically from and for UX research practice.

The most actionable near-term contribution the field could make is a UXR-specific governance framework developed empirically from practitioner needs — analogous to what TITAN provides for academic research reporting.

### Utilizing Generative AI Responsibly and Ethically for Research Purposes in Higher Education: A Policy Analysis

*Alduais, A., et al. (2025). Serials Review.*

**Summary**

Analysis of 74 policy documents from governments, higher education institutions, academic publishers, and publication manuals identifies five core governance domains: transparency and disclosure, authorship and accountability, quality assurance and safety, data security and privacy, and AI literacy and training. The study finds convergence on transparency as foundational but significant variation in implementation specifics. Key gaps identified: equity and inclusivity are underaddressed, and practical enforcement mechanisms are largely absent.

**Relevance to GenAI in UX Research**

Provides a comparative cross-sector governance analysis whose five core domains can serve as a structural scaffold for developing UXR-specific AI governance frameworks.

## Section 6: Open Questions & Gaps

### Methodological Gaps

- **Absence of longitudinal research** — The field lacks multi-year studies tracking how researcher expertise, insight quality, and professional identity evolve with sustained AI tool adoption. Without this, we cannot distinguish productive skill extension from problematic skill erosion.
- **Lab vs. naturalistic settings** — Most studies use controlled or experimental settings. Research in naturalistic UX practice contexts is rare, and lab findings may not transfer.
- **Insight quality measurement** — The field lacks agreed-upon metrics for 'insight quality.' This makes it impossible to test empirically whether AI assistance improves or degrades research output quality beyond efficiency measures.

### Population/Context Gaps

- **Non-Western and non-English-language contexts** — The literature is heavily dominated by US, European, and East Asian studies. GenAI tools built on English-language training data perform in non-English UX research contexts in almost entirely unstudied ways.
- **Small research teams and solo practitioners** — Studies tend to examine enterprise UX teams or academic researchers. Independent UX researchers and small teams — who often lack organizational support for governance — are underrepresented.
- **Domain-specific UX research contexts** — How AI assistance performs differently in specialized contexts (healthcare UX, safety-critical systems, accessibility research) is not well understood.

### Conceptual/Theoretical Gaps

- **UXR-specific governance frameworks** — No peer-reviewed governance framework exists that addresses the specific ethical challenges of AI-assisted user research. This is the field's most urgent practical gap.
- **The researcher role under sustained AI adoption** — What does UX research expertise look like when AI handles volume processing? How do researchers develop and maintain interpretive capabilities? These questions are discussed but not empirically tested.
- **Trust calibration as a skill** — Multiple papers identify appropriate AI trust as critical, but no model exists for how this skill develops or how to train for it.
